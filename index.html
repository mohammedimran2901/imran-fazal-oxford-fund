✦ About

Scholar & Public Servant

Imran Fazal is an Oxford-trained philosopher and traditionally trained Islamic jurist who has dedicated his career to bridging the Islamic scholarly tradition with rigorous academic analysis and public service.

After reading Philosophy, Politics and Economics at Oxford (followed by a B.Phil in Philosophy), he spent a decade in full-time traditional Islamic studies in Damascus, Syria, studying under senior scholars and receiving teaching licences (ijāzāt) in Islamic law, theology, and hadith. He later taught uṣūl al-fiqh (legal theory) to students and graduates of al-Azhar University in Cairo.

In public service, he held senior roles at the UK Home Office and Ministry of Justice, including Deputy Head of the Joint Extremism Unit and Head of Assessments for the Extremism Analysis Unit — roles requiring deep engagement with primary Arabic sources on Islamic legal and theological thought.

✦ The Research

Ibn Taymiyya's Doctrine of Jihad

DPhil Research Proposal — Faculty of Asian and Middle Eastern Studies

Imran's doctoral project aims to study the doctrine of jihād in the writings of the medieval Muslim jurist and theologian Ibn Taymiyya (d. 1328). Despite Ibn Taymiyya's enormous influence on modern militant ideology, there exists no dedicated comprehensive study of his overall doctrine of jihad.

The project seeks to show that later in his life, Ibn Taymiyya argued that war is justified only on grounds of self-defence or inability to practise the Islamic faith freely — a significant departure from prevailing medieval juristic views. This research has profound implications for contemporary counter-extremism and interfaith understanding.

Supervisors

  • Professor Nicolai Sinai FBA — Professor of Islamic Studies, University of Oxford, Fellow of Pembroke College
  • Professor Fitzroy Morrissey — Fellow of All Souls College, Oxford

Key Research Questions

  • What does prior Sunni legal doctrine say about the grounds for initiating war?
  • What are Ibn Taymiyya's views on these questions, and do they depart from the tradition?
  • How do his hermeneutical commitments inform his legal positions?
  • What is the subsequent reception of his views in modern Islamic thought?
